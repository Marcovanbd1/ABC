Neighbourhood Signals

Civic infrastructure for neighbourhood listening.
A public constitutional object that enables a place to listen, at neighbourhood scale, to patterns of pressure and need, and to publish disciplined Neighbourhood Briefs for accountable local stewardship and action.

What kind of thing this is

Neighbourhood Signals is not a product, platform, programme, dataset, or dashboard.
It is a public constitutional object, a civic reference that can be cited, shared, trusted, and defended.

It exists to give places a legitimate way to listen to what is happening as pressure forms, and to respond earlier, more proportionately, and more humanely.

The Framework

The space before demand

The space before demand is the everyday civic terrain in which people are trying to get their bearings before they enter formal systems.

It is where confusion, friction, unmet need, and early distress become visible through everyday help-seeking and navigation.

This space exists in every place. It is real. It carries patterns of pressure long before services feel them. Neighbourhood Signals exists to listen to this space.

What a signal is

A signal is a privacy-safe, aggregated, non-identifying indication that a pattern of pressure, friction, or unmet need is emerging in the space before demand.

Signals belong to neighbourhoods, not to people.

Anything that identifies, tracks, profiles, scores, or targets individuals is not a signal under this constitution.

Red lines

Neighbourhood Signals must never:

  1. Enable identification, tracking, or targeting of individuals or households
  2. Imply that people are being watched, profiled, or scored
  3. Be used for enforcement, compliance monitoring, or investigation
  4. Be packaged, sold, or represented as a proprietary product or vendor system
  5. Allow any organisation to claim ownership of a place’s Signals layer
  6. Introduce paywalls, logins, or user accounts for neighbourhood listening
  7. Produce league tables, rankings, or competitive comparisons
  8. Be used for performance management, target setting, or blame allocation
  9. Present outputs as dashboards, vanity metrics, or abstract analytics
  10. Become a triage system, referral engine, or case management tool
  11. Replace professional judgement, safeguarding processes, or statutory pathways
  12. Centralise control away from local civic stewardship
  13. Bypass local governance
  14. Be implemented covertly or without public civic visibility
  15. Be used for political campaigning or electoral advantage
  16. Be used for commercial targeting, advertising, or lead generation

Anything that violates these red lines is not Neighbourhood Signals.

Interpretation and dispute

Neighbourhood Signals is governed as a constitutional object.

If there is disagreement about interpretation, publication, or action, the default is caution: publish uncertainties, prefer reversible steps, and protect trust over speed.

If there is disagreement about whether something crosses a red line, it is treated as a red line issue and escalated to stewardship. The burden of proof sits with the party proposing expansion.

Where trust is at risk, Guardianship may pause publication pending review.

The Anatomy

Neighbourhood Signals does not live in software. It lives in the civic body of a place.

These are civic functions, not prescribed roles.
A place may hold them within existing people, organisations, and structures, provided they are publicly accountable and independent of vendors.

A place must be able to perform all of the following functions for neighbourhood listening to be legitimate.

Stewardship

Holding the trust.

Someone, or some body, must hold the civic trust of neighbourhood listening.

They safeguard the red lines, protect public legibility, and remain accountable to the place for what neighbourhood listening becomes, and what it does not become.

This function may not be held by a vendor or delivery contractor.

Interpretation

Making sense, not just measuring.

Someone must be able to read neighbourhood patterns through local civic understanding.

They ensure that what is seen is interpreted proportionately, humanely, and in context, rather than mechanically or abstractly.

Convening

Turning listening into shared response.

Someone must be able to bring legitimate local actors together around what the neighbourhood is carrying.

Neighbourhood Signals does not act. Places act, together.

Custodianship

Holding the technical boundaries.

Someone must hold the privacy, aggregation, and neighbourhood boundaries that make listening legitimate.

This function keeps listening safe, bounded, and continuous.

Guardianship

Protecting trust.

Someone must be able to challenge, pause, or stop neighbourhood listening if it drifts, is misused, or risks harming public trust.

Public voice

Keeping listening visible.

Someone must ensure that neighbourhood listening remains publicly legible through Neighbourhood Briefs, so the place can see what it is carrying and what it is doing.

Independent assurance

Making sure it stays true.

Someone independent must be able to scrutinise the layer, review compliance with the red lines, and provide public assurance.

If a place cannot name where these functions sit, it does not yet have neighbourhood listening, it has analytics.

The Acts

Neighbourhood Signals speaks only through Neighbourhood Briefs.

No dashboards, feeds, or analytics outputs constitute lawful civic voice.

The Standing Brief

Issued at a locally declared civic cadence.

The Emergent Brief

Issued when patterns indicate rising or unusual pressure.

Briefs may contain only neighbourhood-level pattern descriptions, civic implications, and proportionate recommendations.

They may never contain individual data, rankings, dashboards, or targeting.

Every Brief must include a route for civic challenge and correction.

Briefs must distinguish observation from interpretation, and remain contestable by the place they describe.

On Value

Neighbourhood Signals introduces a civic capacity, the ability of a place to listen to, interpret, and steward the space before demand.

Its value is understood through what earlier, more proportionate civic action makes possible.

The worth of neighbourhood listening is determined locally, because the pressures, costs of escalation, and civic response capacity of each place are different.

On Travel

Neighbourhood Signals travels by civic enablement, not commercial sale.

Places receive it, steward it, and may enable others to do the same.

No place may require payment, licensing, or dependency as a condition of adoption.

The Founding Clause

Neighbourhood Signals originates in a place.

The first place to steward neighbourhood listening becomes the Founding Steward.

The Founding Steward does not own Neighbourhood Signals. It holds the first civic trust for its stewardship and may never be charged for its own neighbourhood listening.

Amendment

This constitution may evolve, but it may not be weakened.

Amendments must be public, versioned, and accompanied by a plain-language explanation of what changed and why.

No amendment may dilute the red lines, introduce surveillance capability, permit targeting, or reduce public legibility.

Places may add stricter local clauses. They may not add looser ones.

On Legitimate Use of the Name

A place may only claim the name Neighbourhood Signals if it adopts this constitution, honours the red lines, maintains the full civic anatomy, and issues only lawful Neighbourhood Briefs.

The Stewardship Resolution

A place becomes a steward of Neighbourhood Signals through a public Stewardship Resolution.

That resolution must:

  • Adopt this constitution
  • Commit to the red lines
  • Establish the civic anatomy
  • Name accountable stewardship
  • Commit to public legibility through Neighbourhood Briefs

Without such a resolution, no place may legitimately claim stewardship.
